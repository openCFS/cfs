\section{Description}
In  computational aero-acoustics  the so-called  hybrid methods  are quite
common.  These methods try to  extract source terms for acoustics from CFD
data.   There exits  a wide  variety of  such methods.   The  volume based
approach  used  by \cfs\footnote{\cfs\,  is  a  FEM  solver developed  for
  research purposes at LSE Erlangen  and the Chair of Applied Mechatronics
  at Klagenfurt.\\  There exists also  a commercial version  called \nacs~
  which  is distributed  by  Simetris GmbH  \url{http://www.simetris.de}}~
\cite{Kaltenbacher2005a} is the computation of Lighthill source terms in a
FEM framework directly on the the  CFD mesh. Since the CFD mesh is usually
too fine to  perform an acoustic computation on it,  the source terms have
to be conservatively interpolated to a dedicated acoustic grid.  The first
step is the job of \cplreader. The interpolation is performed within \cfs.

The  source term  generator \cplreader\,is  used to  read CFD  meshes from
codes              like              \CFX\footnote{ANSYS              CFX,
  \url{http://www.ansys.com/Products/cfx.asp}},
\OpenFOAM\footnote{OpenFOAM,  \url{http://www.opencfd.co.uk/openfoam}}  or
\FASTEST\footnote{Flow solver FASTEST-3D  developed at LSTM Erlangen} with
the  associated velocity,  pressure  and density  fields,  to compute  the
source terms and writing them to \hdf\footnote{Hierarchical Data Format 5,
  \url{http://hdf.ncsa.uiuc.edu/HDF5}} files.

Aside  from its  primary designated  use \cplreader\,can  also be  used to
convert meshes  from the \ANSYS\,mesh  generator to the  \hdf\,file format
for use in \cfs\,or \nacs.

\section{Theory}

The contents  of this section have been  taken or are based  upon in large
parts from \cite{Kaltenbacher2005}.

\subsection{Introduction}
A  large amount  of the  total noise  in our  daily life  is  generated by
turbulent  flows   (e.g.   airplanes,  cars,   air  conditioning  systems,
etc.). The physics behind the  generation process is quite complicated and
still not fully  understood. The use of numerical  simulation tools is one
important   way    to   analyze    the   generation   of    flow   induced
sound. \figref{fe-setup} displays the  general situation for the numerical
simulation,   we  are  concerned   with,  when   performing  computational
aeroacoustics. Therein $\Omega_1$  denotes the computational domain, where
we  have to solve  for the  flow field  and the  generation of  sound, and
$\Omega_2$ the domain where the flow  is zero, and where we are interested
in the radiated sound.

\begin{figure}[htb]
\begin{center}
\vspace*{5mm} 
\includegraphics[width=10cm,angle=0]{pics/acousticfluidregions}
\caption{General setup of aeroacoustics \label{fig:fe-setup}}
\end{center}
\end{figure}

\noindent
The surface  $\Gamma_{\rm S}$  defines the interaction  between structural
mechanics  and  flow  and  $\Gamma_{\rm  I}$ the  boundary  of  the  total
computational  domain. Now,  the  computation of  the  flow induced  noise
exhibits a  lot of  difficulties, where the  most significant ones  can be
summarized as follows:
\smallskip

\begin{enumerate}
\item The  scale discrepancy between  flow pressure and  acoustic pressure
  reaches values of $10^7$.

\item The  acoustic domain is  in most cases  large compared to  the fluid
  flow domain.

\item The spatial discretization for the flow computation is much finer as
  compared to the discretization of the acoustic domain.

\item In  many applications, the interface between  flow and solid/elastic
  bodies leads  to vibrations,  which not only  effect the flow,  but also
  emits acoustic sound (vibrational acoustics).
\end{enumerate}
\smallskip

\noindent
Due  to  1-3,  a  direct  numerical  simulation  (DNS)  of  Navier  Stokes
equations, which are capable to describe both the flow as well as acoustic
field,   is  restricted   to   very  few   practical   cases  (see   e.g.,
\cite{Tam1995a}).  Therefore,  most approaches,  published in the  last 20
years,  uses hybrid  methods,  e.g.  \cite{Tam1995},  \cite{Colonius2000},
\cite{Ewert2001}.   Since we  concentrate on  a  computational scheme,
which allows  the computation of  the flow generated  sound simultaneously
with any flow induced vibrational sound (due to solid-fluid interactions),
we       base      our       scheme      on       Lighthill's      analogy
\cite{Lighthill1952,Lighthill1954}. This formulation will provide a direct
coupling between  flow and acoustic  sound computation in the  time domain
and   in  addition  will   offer  for   both  physical   fields  different
discretization.  At this point, we want to notice, that a similar approach
can  be   found  in  \cite{Oberai2000},   which  however  has   just  been
investigated in the time-harmonic case.

The outline of this theory section is  as follows: First of all, we give a
review   of  Lighthill's  analogy   (Sec.  \ref{sec:lighthill})   and  its
generalization.  In Sec. \ref{sec:fe} we will then concentrate on the weak
formulation   of  Lighthill's   analogy  and   its  finite   element  (FE)
formulation.   Section  \ref{sec:scheme}  provides  a description  of  our
environment for computational aeroacoustics.

\subsection{Lighthill's Analogy and its Extension}
\label{sec:lighthill}

Let us start our derivation  of Lighthill's analogy by introducing the two
main  equations   of  fluid  dynamics:  mass   conservation  and  momentum
conservation. The differential and coordinate-free formulation of the mass
conservation (continuity) equation reads as follows

\be{eq:massCon}  \frac{\partial  \rho}{\partial   t}  +  \frac{\partial  \rho
  v_i}{\partial x_i} = 0\,.
\ee

\noindent
In (\eqnref{massCon}) $\rho$  denotes the density of the  fluid and $v_i$ the
$i$-th  component  of  the   flow  velocity  vector  $\bv$.  The  momentum
conservation equation is expressed by

\be{eq:momentum1}
\rho\frac{\partial v_i}{\partial t} + \rho v_j \frac{\partial v_i}{\partial x_j} = 
- \frac{\partial p}{\partial x_i} - \frac{\partial \tau_{ij}}{\partial x_j} =
- \frac{\partial}{\partial  x_j} \left( p\delta_{ij} + \tau_{ij} \right)\,,
\ee

\noindent
where $p$ denotes the total pressure within the flow, $[\tau]$ the viscous
stress tensor and $\delta$ the Kronecker symbol.  For Newtonian fluids, we
can express $[\tau]$ by

\be{eq:vst}
\tau_{ij} = - \mu \left( \frac{\partial v_i}{\partial x_j} +
  \frac{\partial v_j}{\partial x_i} \right)
+ \frac{2}{3} \mu \delta_{ij}  \frac{\partial v_k}{\partial x_k} 
\ee

\noindent
with $\mu$ the dynamic viscosity.
% and $[e]$ the rate
%of strain (deformation) tensor computed as follows
%\be{eq:st}
%e_{ij} = \frac{1}{2}\ \left( \frac{\partial v_i}{\partial x_j} + 
% \frac{\partial v_j}{\partial x_i} \right)\,.
%\ee

In  a  first  step,  we  multiply  (\eqnref{massCon})  by  $v_i$  (before  we
substitute  the index  $i$ by  $j$) and  add the  so obtained  equation to
(\eqnref{momentum1}). In addition, we  substitute $p$ in (\eqnref{momentum1}) by
$p - p_0$  with $p_0$ the mean pressure. This  operation is allowed, since
the relation $\partial (p-p_0) /  \partial x_j = \partial p /\partial x_j$
holds. Therewith, we obtain

\be{eq:mom2}
\underbrace{ \rho\frac{\partial v_i}{\partial t} + v_i \frac{\partial
    \rho}{\partial t} }_{\displaystyle\frac{\partial \rho v_i}{\partial t}}
+ \underbrace{\rho v_j \frac{\partial v_i}{\partial x_j} + v_i
  \frac{\partial \rho v_j}{\partial x_j}}_{\displaystyle\frac{\partial }{\partial
    x_j} (\rho v_i v_j)}
= -\frac{\partial}{\partial x_j} ( (p-p_0) \delta_{ij} - \tau_{ij} )
\ee

\noindent
By introducing the momentum flux tensor $[\tau^{\rm I} ]$ by

\be{eq:mflux}
\tau^{\rm I}_{ij} = \rho v_i v_j + (p-p_0) \delta_{ij} - \tau_{ij}
\ee

\noindent
we may write (\eqnref{mom2}) as

\be{eq:monmentum2}
 \frac{\partial \rho v_i}{\partial t} = - \frac{\partial \tau^{\rm I}_{ij}}{\partial x_j}\,.
\ee

\noindent
For linear acoustics, the momentum flux tensor $[\tau^{\rm I}]$ reduces to

\be{eq:tau0}
\tau_{ij}^0 = (p-p_0)\delta_{ij} 
\ee

\noindent
and (\eqnref{monmentum2}) reads as

\be{eq:monmentum3}
 \frac{\partial \rho v_i}{\partial t}  + \frac{\partial}{\partial x_i}
 \left( p - p_0 \right) = 0\,. 
\ee

\noindent
In addition,  we define the  acoustic pressure $p'$, the  acoustic density
$\rho'$ and its relation (just fulfilled for linear acoustics)

\be{eq:acoustics}
p' = p - p_0 \,; \ \ \ \rho' = \rho - \rho_0 \,; \ \ \
\frac{p'}{\rho'} = c_0^2
\ee

\noindent
with $c_0$ the speed  of sound. Substituting $p-p_0$ in (\eqnref{monmentum3})
by $c_0^2 \rho'$ and applying  $\partial / \partial x_i$ to this equation,
results in

\be{eq:contxx}
\frac{\partial }{\partial t} \frac{\partial }{\partial x_i} ( \rho
v_i) + \frac{\partial^2 }{\partial x_i^2} (c_0^2 \rho') = 0\,.
\ee

\noindent
According   to   the   mass   conservation  equation,   we   may   rewrite
(\eqnref{contxx}) as

\be{eq:we1}
\frac{\partial^2 \rho'}{\partial t^2} + \frac{\partial^2
}{\partial x_i^2} (c_0^2 \rho') =
\left( \frac{1}{c_0^2} \,  \frac{\partial^2}{\partial t^2} - \frac{\partial^2
}{\partial x_i^2}\right) (c_0^2 \rho')  
= 0 \,.
\ee

\noindent
Since we  have neglected the flow  and there are no  other excitations, no
acoustic sound field will be  generated, since the solution of (\eqnref{we1})
is $\rho' = \rho - \rho_0 = 0$.\\

Lighthill's analogy is based on the  fact, that the sound generated by the
flow in a real fluid is  exactly equivalent to that produced in the ideal,
linear acoustic field, forced by the stress distribution

\begin{eqnarray}
\label{eq:Lt1}
L_{ij} &=& \tau^{\rm I}_{ij} - \tau_{ij}^0 \nonumber \\
&=& \rho v_i v_j + \left( (p-p_0) - c_0^2 (\rho - \rho_0)\right) \delta_{ij} - \tau_{ij}\,,
\end{eqnarray}

\noindent
where $[L]$ denotes the {Lighthill stress tensor}.

We can  now rewrite  (\eqnref{monmentum2}) as the  momentum equation  for the
ideal, linear  acoustic medium subjected to the  externally applied stress
according to (\eqnref{Lt1})

\be{eq:momentum2m1}
\frac{\partial \rho v_i}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial \tau_{ij}^0}{\partial x_j} =
- \frac{\partial (\tau^{\rm I}_{ij} - \tau_{ij}^0)}{\partial x_j}
\ee

\noindent
Therewith, we obtain

\be{eq:momentum2m2}
\frac{\partial \rho v_i}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial}{\partial x_i} \left(c_0^2(\rho - \rho_0)\right)
= - \frac{\partial L_{ij}}{\partial x_j}\,.
\ee

\noindent
Eliminating the  momentum density $\rho  v_i$ similarly as for  the linear
case, we obtain

\be{eq:L1}
\left( \frac{1}{c_0^2} \,  \frac{\partial^2}{\partial t^2} - \frac{\partial^2
}{\partial x_i^2} \right) (c_0^2(\rho - \rho_0)) 
= \frac{\partial^2 L_{ij}}{\partial x_i \partial x_j}\,.
\ee

\noindent
The integral formulation of (\eqnref{L1}) is given by

\be{eq:L1I}
c_0^2 (\rho - \rho_0) (\bx,t) = \frac{1}{4\pi} \, \frac{\partial^2}{\partial x_i \partial x_j}
\, \int\limits_{\Omega_1}\, \frac{\partial L_{ij}(\bxi, t - r/c_0)}{r}\, \rd\xi \rd\eta \rd\zeta
\ee

\noindent
with the source coordinates $\bxi = (\xi, \eta, \zeta)^T$, the observation
coordinates  $\bx =  (x_1,x_2,  x_3)^T$  and the  distance  vector $\br  =
(x_1-\xi, x_2-\eta, x_3-\zeta)^T$.\\

The integral formulation given in (\eqnref{L1I}) is no longer correct, if any
solid and/or  elastic bodies  (in rest or  moving) are present  within the
simulation domain. For such situations,  the acoustic field has to fulfill
the correct boundary  condition and we no longer  can use Green's function
for  free radiation.   Now, the  idea is,  to derive  an extended  form of
Lighthill's equation, which is defined in the whole simulation domain, and
thus  Green's function  for free  radiation can  be applied  to  obtain an
integral formulation \cite{Ffowcs-Williams1969}.

%This idea, which will be applied in the
%following, can be also interpreted as using the principle of
%mirroring (see e.g. \cite{}). Therewith, we introduce fictive acoustic
%sources, which are mirrored sources to the original ones, and do not
%model solid boundaries.

We will  assume a domain  with a solid  body, which is located  within the
considered  domain  $\Omega_1$. The  volume  of  the  body is  denoted  by
$\Omega_{\rm   B}$   and   its    surface   by   $\Gamma_{\rm   S}$   (see
\figref{fe-setup}). We  describe the surface  of the body with  a function
$f(\bx, t)$

\be{eq:G1}
f(\bx,t) = 
\left\{ 
\begin{array}{ll}
f < 0 & \hbox{for } \bx \in \Omega_{\rm B} \\[2mm]
f > 0 & \hbox{for } \bx \in \Omega_1 \setminus \Omega_{\rm B} \\[2mm]
f = 0 & \hbox{for } \bx \hbox{ on } \Gamma_{\rm S} 
\end{array}
\right.
\ee

\noindent
e.g.,   a   breathing  sphere:   $f(\bx,t)   =   |   \bx  -   \bx_o|^2   -
R_0^2(t)$. Additionally, we introduce the Heaviside function $H(f(\bx,t))$

\be{eq:H1}
H(f(\bx,t)) = 
\left\{ 
\begin{array}{ll}
1  & \hbox{for } \bx \in  \Omega_1 \setminus \Omega_{\rm B} \\[2mm]
0  & \hbox{for } \bx \in \Omega_{\rm B}\ \ \,.
\end{array}
\right.
\ee

\noindent
First of  all, we multiply  the continuity equation  (see (\eqnref{massCon}))
with $H(f)$

\be{eq:eL1}
\left( \frac{\partial \rho'}{\partial t} + 
\frac{\partial}{\partial x_i} (\rho v_i) \right) H(f) = 0
\ee

\noindent
and rewrite it in the following form

\begin{eqnarray}
\label{eq:Lex1}
\frac{\partial}{\partial t} \left( \rho' H(f) \right)
- \rho' \frac{\partial H(f) }{\partial t}  
+ \frac{\partial}{\partial t} \left( (\rho v_i) H(f) \right)
-
(\rho v_i) \frac{\partial H(f) }{\partial x_i} 
= 0\,.
\end{eqnarray}
%
\smallskip

\noindent
The   Heaviside  function   $H(f)$  fulfills   two   important  properties
\cite{Howe2002}

\begin{eqnarray}
\label{eq:Hp1}
\frac{\partial H(f) }{\partial x_i}  &=& \frac{\partial f}{\partial
  x_i} \delta(f)\\
\label{eq:Hp2}
\frac{\partial H(f) }{\partial t} &=& \delta(f) \, \frac{\partial
  f}{\partial t}  = - v^{\rm B}_i \frac{\partial f}{\partial
  x_i} \delta(f)
\end{eqnarray}
\smallskip

\noindent
with $v^{\rm  B}_i$ the  velocity of the  solid body in  $\Omega_1$. Using
(\eqnref{Hp1}) and (\eqnref{Hp2}) simplifies (\eqnref{Lex1}) to

\be{eq:eLce1}
\frac{\partial (\rho' H(f))}{\partial t} 
+ \frac{\partial  (\rho v_i H(f)) }{\partial x_i} 
= \left( \rho (v_i - v^{\rm B}_i) + \rho_0 v^{\rm B}_i \right) \frac{\partial f}{\partial
  x_i} \delta(f)\,.
\ee

\noindent
This  equation  is an  extension  of  the  continuity equation,  which  is
fulfilled  all over  $\Omega$. As  can  be seen,  the right  hand side  of
(\eqnref{eLce1}) is just on $\Gamma_{\rm S}$ different from zero and the left
side is zero within $\Omega_{\rm  B}$.  Applying similar operations to the
momentum conservation equation, we obtain

\begin{eqnarray}
\label{eq:eLmo1}
\frac{\partial}{\partial t} \left( \rho v_i H(f) \right)
&+& \frac{\partial}{\partial x_j} \left( \rho v_i v_j +
  p'\delta_{ij}
- \tau_{ij} \right) H(f)\nonumber \\
&=&
\left( \rho v_i (v_j - v^{\rm B}_j) + p' \delta_{ij}- \tau_{ij} \right) 
\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_j} \delta(f)\,.
\end{eqnarray}

\noindent
Using the  definition of the Lighthill tensor  $L_{ij}$ (see (\eqnref{Lt1})),
we may write (\eqnref{eLmo1}) as

\begin{eqnarray}
\label{eq:eLmo2}
\frac{\partial}{\partial t} \left( \rho v_i H(f) \right)
&+& \frac{\partial }{\partial x_i} (c_0^2 \rho' H(f)) \nonumber \\
&=& -\frac{\partial L_{ij} H(f)}{\partial x_j} 
 +\left( \rho v_i (v_j - v^{\rm B}_j) + p' \delta_{ij}- \tau_{ij} \right) 
\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_j} \delta(f)\,. \ \ \ \ \ \ \ 
\end{eqnarray}

\noindent
Furthermore, we rewrite (\eqnref{eLce1})  in the following form

\be{eq:eLce1r}
\frac{\partial (\rho v_i H(f))}{\partial t} 
 = \left( \rho (v_i - v^{\rm B}_i) + \rho_0 v^{\rm B}_i \right) \frac{\partial f}{\partial
  x_i} \delta(f) 
- \frac{\partial  (\rho' H(f)) }{\partial x_i}  \,.
\ee

\noindent
Applying $\partial /\partial x_i$  to (\eqnref{eLmo2}) and using the relation
according to (\eqnref{eLce1r}), we arrive at the extended form of Lighthill's
equation

\begin{eqnarray}
\label{eq:eLend}
&&\left( \frac{1}{c_0^2}\, \frac{\partial^2}{\partial t^2} - \frac{\partial^2
}{\partial x_i^2} \right)
\left( c_0^2 \rho'\, H(f) \right) \nonumber \\
&=&
\frac{\partial^2}{\partial x_i \partial x_j} \left( L_{ij} H(f)
\right)  - \frac{\partial }{\partial x_i} \left( \left( \rho v_i (v_j -
    v^{\rm B}_j)   +
p'\, \delta_{ij} - \tau_{ij} \right) \frac{\partial f}{\partial
x_j} \delta(f) \right) \nonumber \\
&&+
 \frac{\partial}{\partial t} \left(  \left(\rho(v_i - v^{\rm B}_i) + \rho_0
    v^{\rm B}_i \right) \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i} \delta(f) \right)\,.
\end{eqnarray}

\noindent
Since the  extended form of  Lighthill's equation is valid  throughout the
whole domain $\Omega$,  we can use Green's function  for free radiation to
obtain a  solution. Therefore,  the integral form,  which is known  as the
{\it  Ffowcs Williams  and Hawkings  equation} \cite{Ffowcs-Williams1969},
reads as

\begin{eqnarray}
 \rho'\, H(f) = \frac{\partial^2}{\partial x_i \partial x_j} \,
\int\limits_{\Omega_1}\, \frac{\partial L_{ij}}{4\pi\, r}\,d\Omega
&-& \frac{\partial }{\partial x_i} \, \oint\limits_{\Gamma_{\rm S}}\, 
\frac{ \left(\rho(v_i - v^{\rm B}_i) + p'\, \delta_{ij} - \tau_{ij}
  \right)}{4\pi\, r} \,\rd\Gamma \nonumber\\
&+& \frac{\partial }{\partial t} \, \oint\limits_{\Gamma_{\rm S}}\,
\frac{\left(\rho(v_i - v^{\rm B}_i) + \rho
    v^{\rm B}_i \right)}{4\pi \, r} \rd\Gamma\,.
\end{eqnarray}

\subsection{Finite Element Formulation}
\label{sec:fe}

We  will perform  a  volume discretization  of  Lighthill's equation  (see
(\eqnref{L1})) by  applying the finite  element method (FEM).  Therewith, any
solid/elastic body will be implicitly  taken into account, and there is no
need  to  use  the extended  form  of  Lighthill's  equation as  given  by
(\eqnref{eLend}).

Let  us start  at the  strong formulation  of (\eqnref{L1}),  which  reads as
follows

\[
\begin{array}{lll}
\hbox{Given:}& L_{ij}&: \Omega \times (0,T) \rightarrow {\R}\\
&c_0&: \Omega  \rightarrow {\R}\\[2mm]
&\rho'^0&: \Omega  \rightarrow {\R}\\[2mm]
&\dot \rho'^0&: \Omega  \rightarrow {\R}\\[2mm]
\hbox{Find:} &\rho'&: \bar{\Omega} \times [0,T] \rightarrow
{\R}
\end{array}
\]
\vspace*{-4mm}

\begin{center}
\begin{eqnarray}
\label{eq:strongF}
\frac{\partial^2 \rho'}{\partial t^2} - c_0^2  \frac{\partial^2 \rho'}{\partial x_i^2} 
&=& \frac{\partial^2 L_{ij}}{\partial x_i \partial x_j}\\
\rho'(\br,0) &=& {\rho'}^0 \, , \ \br \in \Omega \nonumber \\
\dot \rho' (\br,0) &=& \dot {\rho'}^0 \, , \ \br \in \Omega \nonumber 
\end{eqnarray}
\end{center}

\noindent
At  this stage  we assume,  that  the Lighthill  tensor $[L]$  is a  known
quantity  obtained by  a flow  computation using  a large  eddy simulation
(LES)  or other  appropriate  type of  CFD  scheme. In  a  first step,  we
multiply (\eqnref{strongF}) by an appropriate test function $w$ and integrate
over the  whole domain $\Omega$ (corresponding  in \figref{fe-setup}
to $\Omega_1 \cup \Omega_2$)

\be{eq:wL1}
\int\limits_\Omega \,
\, w\left( \frac{\partial^2 \rho'}{\partial t^2} -
    c_0^2\,\frac{\partial^2 \rho'}{\partial x_i^2} - \frac{\partial^2
      L_{ij}}{\partial x_i \partial x_j} \right)\rd\Omega = 0\,.
\ee

\noindent
Now, we apply Green's integral theorem to the second spatial derivative of
$\rho'$ as well  as $L_{ij}$. This operation will  result in the following
relations

\begin{eqnarray}
\label{eq:Green1}
\int_\Omega c_0^2\, w  \frac{\partial^2 \rho'}{\partial x_i^2} \, \rd \Omega &=&
\int_{\Gamma_{\rm S} \cup \Gamma_{\rm I}}\, w \frac{\partial \rho'}{\partial n}\, \rd \Gamma
- \int_\Omega c_0^2\, \frac{\partial w}{\partial x_i} \, \frac{\partial
  \rho'}{\partial x_i} \, \rd \Omega \\[2mm] 
\label{eq:Green2}
\int_\Omega \, w\, \frac{\partial^2 L_{ij}}{\partial x_i \partial x_j} \, \rd \Omega &=&
\int_{\Gamma_{\rm S}}\, w \frac{\partial L_{ij}}{\partial x_j} n_i\, \rd \Gamma
- \int_\Omega \frac{\partial w}{\partial x_i} \frac{\partial
  L_{ij}}{\partial x_j}\, \rd \Omega\,. 
\end{eqnarray}

\noindent
We want  to emphasize, that  the boundary integral (\eqnref{Green2})  is just
over the  surface $\Gamma_{\rm S}$  of any solid/elastic body,  whereas in
(\eqnref{Green1}) we have to integrate  over $\Gamma_{\rm S}$ as well as over
$\Gamma_{\rm   I}$,   which   limits   the   computational   domain   (see
\figref{fe-setup}).

Now we  can substitute $\partial L_{ij}  / \partial x_j$  within the first
term on the  right hand side of (\eqnref{Green2})  by (\eqnref{momentum2m2}) and
obtain

\be{eq:wL3}
\int_{\Gamma_{\rm S}}\, w \frac{\partial L_{ij}}{\partial x_j} n_i\,
\rd \Gamma
= \int_{\Gamma_{\rm S}}\, w\, \left( - \frac{\partial \rho
    v_i}{\partial t} - \frac{\partial}{\partial x_i} (c_0^2 \rho')
\right)  n_i\,\rd \Gamma\,.
\ee

\noindent
Since on  a solid surface  $v_i n_i  = 0$ is  fulfilled, we see,  that the
surface integral term over $\Gamma_{\rm S}$ reduces to

\be{eq:GammaS}
\int_{\Gamma_{\rm S}}\, w \frac{\partial L_{ij}}{\partial x_j} n_i\,
\rd \Gamma = - \int_{\Gamma_{\rm S}} \, c_0^2 \, w \, \frac{\partial
  \rho'}{\partial n}\, \rd\Gamma\,.
\ee

\noindent
Therewith, we can rewrite (\eqnref{wL1}) as

\begin{eqnarray}
\int_\Omega w \frac{\partial^2 \rho'}{\partial t^2} 
& + & \ \int_\Omega c_0^2\, \frac{\partial w}{\partial x_i} \, \frac{\partial
  \rho'}{\partial x_i} \, \rd \Omega 
\ -\ \int_{\Gamma_{\rm S} \cup \Gamma_{\rm I}}\, c_0^2 \, w
\frac{\partial \rho'}{\partial n}\, \rd \Gamma \nonumber \\[2mm]
&=&
- \int_\Omega \frac{\partial w}{\partial x_i} \frac{\partial
  L_{ij}}{\partial x_j}\, \rd \Omega -
\int_{\Gamma_{\rm S}}\, c_0^2 \, w \frac{\partial \rho'}{\partial n}\, \rd \Gamma
\,.
\end{eqnarray}

\noindent  Combining  the  surface  integrals  results in  a  single  boundary
integral just performed over the  outer boundary $\Gamma_{\rm I}$, on which we
apply   absorbing   boundary   conditions   of  first   order   according   to
\cite{Engquist1977}. Therewith, we utilize the relation

\be{eq:ABC1}
c_0 \frac{\partial \rho'}{\partial n} = \frac{\partial
  \rho'}{\partial t}
\ee

\noindent and  arrive at the  weak form of(\eqnref{strongF}): Find  $\rho' \in
H^1$ such that

\be{eq:weakF}
\int_\Omega w \frac{\partial^2 \rho'}{\partial t^2} 
 +  \ \int_\Omega c_0^2\, \frac{\partial w}{\partial x_i} \, \frac{\partial
  \rho'}{\partial x_i} \, \rd \Omega 
\ -\ \int_{\Gamma_{\rm I}}\, c_0^2 \, w
\frac{\partial \rho'}{\partial n}\, \rd \Gamma \nonumber \\[2mm]
=
- \int_\Omega \frac{\partial w}{\partial x_i} \frac{\partial
  L_{ij}}{\partial x_j}\, \rd \Omega 
\ee

\noindent for any $w \in H^1$.  With $H^1$ we denote the Sobolev space defined
as (see e.g. \cite{Adams1975})

\be{eq:H1space}
H^1 = \{ u \in L^2 | \partial u / \partial x_i \in L^2 \}
\ee

\noindent and $L^2$ the space of square integrable functions.

Using standard  nodal finite elements, we approximate  the continuous acoustic
density $\rho'$ as well as the test function $w$ by

\bea{approx_psi1}
\rho' &\approx & {\rho'}^h =  \sum\limits_{a=1}^{n_{\rm eq}} \,
N_a \rho'_{a} \\
w &\approx & w^h =  \sum\limits_{a=1}^{n_{\rm eq}} \, N_a w_a \,.
\eea

\noindent Thus, (\eqnref{weakF}) is  transformed to the following semidiscrete
Galerkin formulation

\begin{equation}
{\bf M} \underline{\ddot{\rho'}}_{n+1} +
{\bf C} \underline{\dot{\rho'}}_{n+1} +
{\bf K} \underline{\rho'}_{n+1} = \underline f_{n+1}
\end{equation}

\noindent  with $\underline{\ddot{\rho'}}  = \partial^2  \rho_\sim  / \partial
t^2$,   $\underline{\dot{\rho'}}   =  \partial   \rho_\sim   /  \partial   t$,
$\underline{\rho'}$ the  nodal unknowns  of the acoustic  density and  $n$ the
time step counter.  The matrices as well as right hand  side vector compute as
follows:

\bea{acousticmatrices}
\bM &=& \bigwedge\limits_{e=1}^{n_{\rm e}} {\bf m}^e \ \ ; \ \ {\bf m}^e = [ m_{pq}] 
\ \  ; \ \ m_{pq} = \int\limits_{\Omega^e} \, N_p N_q \, \rd\Omega
\\[2mm]
\bC &=& \bigwedge\limits_{e=1}^{n_{\Gamma_{\rm I}}} \bc^e \ \ ; \ \ \bc^e = [ c_{pq}] 
\ \  ; \ \ c_{pq} = \int\limits_{\Gamma_{\rm I}^e} \,c_0\, N_p N_q \, \rd\Gamma \\[2mm]
\bK &=& \bigwedge\limits_{e=1}^{n_\rme} \bk^e \ \ ; \ \ \bk^e = [ k_{pq}] %\nonumber\\
\ \  ; \ \ k_{pq} = \int\limits_{\Omega^e} \,c^2 \, \frac{\partial
  N_p}{\partial x_i}\, \frac{\partial  N_q}{\partial x_i}\, \rd\Omega \\[2mm]
\underline f_{n+1} &=& \bigwedge\limits_{e=1}^{n_\rme} \underline f^e
\ \ ; \ \ \underline f^e = [f_p ]
\ \  ; \ \ f_{p} = \int\limits_{\Omega^e} \frac{\partial N_p}{\partial x_i} \frac{\partial
  L_{ij}^{n+1}}{\partial x_j}\, \rd \Omega \,.
\label{eq:RHS_L}
\eea

\noindent In the above equations $n_{\rm e}$ is the number of finite elements,
$n_{\Gamma_{\rm I}}$  the number  of finite surface  elements along  the outer
boundary  $\Gamma_{\rm  I}$  and   $\bigwedge$  the  finite  element  assembly
operator.

\noindent
The  time  discretization is  performed  by  applying  a standard  Newmark
algorithm, which results in  the following time-stepping scheme (effective
mass matrix formulation) \cite{Kaltenbacher2007}:

\begin{itemize}
\item Perform predictor step:
\bea{eq:Psidist1}
\underline{\tilde \rho}' &=& \underline {\rho'}_n + \Delta t \, 
\dot{\underline \rho'}_n + \frac{\Delta t^2}{2} (1-2\beta_{\rm H})\, \ddot{\underline \rho'}_n \\
\underline{\tilde{\dot \rho}}' &=& \dot{\underline \rho'}^n + (1-\gamma_{\rm H}) \Delta t \,
\ddot{\underline \rho'}_n\,.
\eea
\smallskip

\item Solve algebraic system of equations:

\bea{eq:Psidist2}
\bM^* \underline{\ddot \rho'}_{n+1} &=& \underline f_{n+1} - \bK
\underline{\tilde \rho'}
- {\bf C} \tilde {\underline{\dot \rho'}}  \\
\bM^* &=& {\bf M} + \gamma_{\rm H}\Delta t\, {\bf C} +
\beta \Delta t^2\, {\bf K}\,.
\eea
\smallskip

\item Perform corrector step:
\bea{eq:Psitdist3}
\underline{\rho'}_{n+1} &=& {\underline{\tilde \rho}}' + \beta_{\rm H}
\Delta t^2\,\ddot{\underline \rho'}_{n+1} \\ 
\underline{\dot{\rho}'}_{n+1} &=& {\dot{\underline{\tilde \rho'}}} + \gamma_{\rm H} \Delta t
\,\ddot{\underline {\rho'}}_{n+1}\,.
\label{eq:Psitdist31}
\eea

\end{itemize}
\bigskip

\noindent  In (\eqnref{Psidist1}) -  (\eqnref{Psitdist31}) $\Delta  t$ denotes
the  time  step  value  and  $\beta_{\rm  H}$,  $\gamma_{\rm  H}$  integration
parameters, which are set to $0.25$  and $0.5$ to obtain a fully implicit time
integration scheme.

\subsection{Calculation Scheme}
\label{sec:scheme}

We propose a coupling between  flow and acoustic computations, which allows to
choose  an optimal discretization  for each  physical field.  The flow  may be
computed by the codes \CFX, \OpenFOAM, \FASTEST\, \cite{Durst1996}. \FASTEST\,
uses a fully conservative  second-order finite volume space discretization and
a pressure correction method of the  SIMPLE type for the iterative coupling of
velocity  and  pressure.  \CFX\,  uses  the  Scale  Adaptive Simulation  (SAS)
scheme.  \OpenFOAM\, is an open source CFD solver.

\begin{figure}[hbt]
\begin{center}
\vspace*{5mm} 
\includegraphics[width=10cm,angle=0]{pics/couplingscheme}
\caption{Coupling Scheme \label{fig:calc-scheme}}
\end{center}
\end{figure}

\noindent For time discretization an implicit second-order scheme is employed,
while a non-linear  multigrid scheme, in which the  pressure correction method
acts  as a  smoother on  the different  grid levels,  is used  for convergence
acceleration. The turbulent flow is computed by an appropriate CFD scheme like
the  large eddy simulation  (LES) applying  the Smagorinsky  model or  the SAS
model  of  \CFX.   The  concept  of  block structured  grids  is  employed  in
\FASTEST\,   to   handle   complex    geometries   and   for   the   ease   of
parallelization.  The parallel  implementation is  based on  grid partitioning
with  automatic  load  balancing  and  follows  the  message-passing  concept,
ensuring a high degree of portability.

The computation  of the  generated acoustic noise  is performed by  the finite
element method as  discussed in Sec. \ref{sec:fe} and  has been implemented in
CFS++  (Coupled  Field  Simulation)  \cite{Kaltenbacher2005a}.   In  order  to
minimize the  amount of  data transfer  and to achieve  a higher  accuracy, we
already compute the right  hand side $\underline f^{n+1}$ (see \eqnref{RHS_L})
on the much  finer flow grid and obtain so called  acoustic nodal loads.  This
is performed  as a post-processing step  of the CFD  computation by cplreader.
Within each  time step, these scalar  values are interpolated  to the acoustic
grid by using  a customized conservative interpolation algorithm  in \cfs\, or
MpCCI  - the  Mesh-based parallel  Code Coupling  Interface  \cite{MpCCI} (see
\figref{calc-scheme}). In doing so the weights for the distribution of the CFD
quantity to  the nodes  in the  acoustic cell are  obtained by  evaluating the
basis functions defined in Tab.   \ref{basis-functions} at the position of the
CFD node. More  technical details on this issue may be  found in Section 6.4.2
in  \cite{MpCCITechRef} which  is  also  available as  part  of the  cplreader
documentation.

\begin{figure}[hbt]
\begin{center}
\vspace*{5mm} 
\includegraphics[width=15cm,angle=0]{pics/cons_interpolation}
\caption{Conservative interpolation of source terms from CFD to acoustic grid. \label{fig:cons-interpolation}}
\end{center}
\end{figure}

\noindent The data transfer has to be performed within the whole computational
domain of the flow (corresponds to $\Omega_1$ in \figref{fe-setup}).  In order
to obtain  a correct interpolation, the  acoustic nodal loads have  then to be
interpolated by  a {\it conservative  technique}, since they correspond  to an
integral quantity.

Therewith, the  developed scheme allows a  direct coupling in  time domain and
provides the acoustic  sound field not only  in the far field but  also in the
region of  the flow.   Furthermore, we can  directly investigate  the acoustic
source terms  in the  flow region and  the scheme  is well suited  for complex
geometries  and fluid structure  interaction. By  applying a  discrete Fourier
transform to the acoustic nodal loads, it is also possible to perform analyses
in the frequency domain.

In addition  to the full 3D coupling,  we have implemented a  2D coupling from
the flow to the acoustic  computation. Therewith, we just compute the acoustic
nodal  loads  on  a  predefined  2D  slice  and  perform  the  acoustic  field
computation only within a cross section. It is also possible to slice a 3D CFD
dataset by  interpolating to a 2D  acoustic grid.  In both  cases the acoustic
nodal loads  have been  previously computed  and written to  a \hdf\,  file by
cplreader.   This  file  has  then   been  read  and  interpolated  by  \cfs\,
(cf.  \figref{cons-interpolation}).  This  technique  allows us  to write  the
computed  acoustic nodal  loads to  a  file and  perform acoustic  simulations
independent of the flow computation.

\subsubsection{Required File Layout}

|   |-- 35445.trn
|   `-- 35446.trn

`-- name.res
\end{verbatim}

All entities 'name' may also be symbolic links.

\subsubsection{Description of the Files}

\begin{description}
  \item[.def]  The  .def file  contains  the  mesh  with informations  on  its
    partitioning.  Unfortunately  the   names  of  the  partitions/regions  as
    specified in CFX and no surface regions can be read from the CFX .def file
    at  the moment.  This would  be highly  desirable for  specifying standard
    acoustic absorbing BCs or  for Mortar coupled simulations. A corresponding
    ticket       has      already       been      submitted       to      Trac
    (cf. \url{https://lse17.e-technik.uni-erlangen.de:2001/trac/ticket/165}).

  \item[.res] The .res file acts as a  result file as well as restart file for
    CFX runs.  It  is the only file needed to restart  a CFX computation. This
    means that, if the frequency  resolution of the harmonic source term field
    is too  low due to too few  .trn files, one can  always produce additional
    .trn steps by restarting the CFX  computation from the .res file.  It also
    contains the mesh  plus the simulation description for the  CFX run in the
    COMMANDS dataset. The  COMMANDS dataset is comparable to  our .xml file in
    addition to  our material file. It  contains such information  as the time
    step size,  which time steps get  written to .trn files  (e.g.  only every
    second step),  a string  dataset with  the file names  of all  .trn files,
    material properties,  boundary conditions,  etc.  For later  reference the
    COMMANDS dataset gets written to  the UserData group of the resulting HDF5
    file.   Since the  .res file  also  contains the  mesh, one  may create  a
    symbolic link name.def to name.res, if a .def file is missing.

    It has been  found however that .res files produced  with CFX version 12.x
    can      cause     problems      with      cplreader     (Trac      ticket
    \url{https://lse17.e-technik.uni-erlangen.de:2001/trac/ticket/165}).     If
    this is the case  one can get rid of the problem  by only reading the mesh
    from the  .def file. The .res file  should be deleted or  renamed for this
    purpose. The list of .trn files is obtained by cplreader by inspecting the
    contents  of  'name' subdirectory  instead  of  reading the  corresponding
    dataset from  the .res file in  this case.  Please note  that the COMMANDS
    dataset  will  not  be  available  in   the  HDF5  file,  if  no  .res  is
    present. Therefore the information about time step size has to be obtained
    in a different manner.

  \item[.trn]  The .trn  files  contain the  actual  field data  for the  time
    steps. The  cplreader tries to read  as much information  as possible from
    the  .trn files  if  the  '--outputfields all'  option  is specified.   In
    addition to  the velocity field which  is required for  the calculation of
    the Lighthill source term, this  includes the turbulent kinetic energy and
    density fields.  There  will however be no crash, if  these fields are not
    present.  If  no --outputfields or  no --calcsrc parameters  are specified
    nothing but the mesh from the .def file will be read at all.
\end{description}

\subsubsection{Special Options}

\begin{description}
  \item[{\tt --type}] The type of input files have to be set to 'CFX'.

  \item[{\tt --deffile}]  You can specify  different '.def' file than  the one
    given by 'name.def'.

  \item[{\tt --trntol}] Percentage the file size of transient files may differ
    from the average file size of  all transient files. If a transient file is
    smaller than average size * (1  - trntol) the corresponding time step will
    be overwritten by the previous one.  This option has been introduced to be
    more robust  against damaged  .trn files, which  may result  during faulty
    data transfers.
\end{description}


\subsubsection{Logging}

Opening the root \hdf file ('name'.h5) one can find in /UserData/CFX\_COMMANDS
information about  the CFX  run which produced  the .res file.   This includes
timestep  size, material  properties, boundary  conditions,  turbulence model,

non-corrupt .trn file.

cplreader --type CFX --name fluid --numsteps 11 --timestep 1e-4 \
          --deffile fluid_old.def --trntol 0.05 --outputfields 'all'
\end{verbatim}

This would  read all transient files  except 6.trn which would  be replaced by
5.trn.  You can even replace the .def  file by the .res file since the grid is
also saved in the .res file.  The transient files get searched by cplreader in
this case.

Please note that no surface elements will be read by the CFX reader.

\subsection{CFX\_EXPORT}
\label{sec:cfx-export}
\subsubsection{Required File Layout}
\begin{verbatim}
./name/
|--results_1.csv
`--results_2.csv
\end{verbatim}

\subsubsection{Required File Format}
Data  is  stored   in  \emph{groups},  that  must  begin   with  a  line  like
\texttt{[Name]}. Group names are case sensitive. Groups not mentioned here are
ignored. Lines that begin with \texttt{\#} are ignored.

\paragraph{\texttt{Data} group}
This group  contains the  coordinates of nodes  as well  as the flow  data per
node.  The  first line  contains the column  headers separated by  comma. Each
column header consists of  a name of a field followed by  its unit enclosed in
brackets, e.g.\ ``\texttt{Pressure [ Pa ]}''. Table~\ref{tab:csv_fields} shows
the recognized  column headers. All following  lines up to the  next group (or
end of file) provide the data using  one line per node and values separated by
comma.

\begin{table}[htb]
\centering
\caption{Data fields supported by CFX\_EXPORT reader}
\label{tab:csv_fields}
\begin{tabular}{lll}
	\textbf{Field name} & \textbf{Unit} & \textbf{Description} \\
	\hline\hline
	\texttt{Node Number} & & non-negative unique integer number identifying the
	node \\
	\hline
	\texttt{X} & \texttt{m} & x coordinate of the node (required) \\
	\hline
	\texttt{Y} & \texttt{m} & y coordinate of the node (required) \\
	\hline
	\texttt{Z} & \texttt{m} & z coordinate of the node \\
	\hline
	\texttt{Density} & \verb|kg m^-3| \\
	\hline
	\texttt{Pressure} & \texttt{Pa} \\
	\hline
	\texttt{Velocity u} & \verb|m s^-1| & x component of velocity in computational
	frame \\
	\hline
	\texttt{Velocity v} & \verb|m s^-1| & y component of velocity in computational
	frame
	\\
	\hline
	\texttt{Velocity w} & \verb|m s^-1| & z component of velocity in computational
	frame \\
	\hline\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\paragraph{\texttt{Faces} group}
This group contains the connectivity of  nodes. There must be no column header
line. Each  line provides  the node numbers  (as defined in  the \texttt{Data}
group) of one finite element, separated by  comma. Elements with 3, 4, 5, or 6
nodes are allowed. Elements with 5 or 6 nodes will be split into triangles.

Although every data file may  contain different node coordinates and different
connectivity, the mesh is only read from the first data file.

\section{Setting up the Interpolation Calculation in CFS++}

In order  to interpolate the  acoustic source terms  from the CFD  mesh, which
cplreader has written  out, to the acoustic mesh of the  source region a CFS++
simulation has to be set up. One needs to have a XML file, a material file and
the  mesh files  for the  acoustic mesh  and the  CFD mesh  with  the acoustic
sources. A typical XML file for doing the interpolation looks like this:

\input{sources/cfs_interpolation}

\noindent Figure  \ref{fig:interpolation_tolerances} shows the  meaning of the
tolerance parameters in the interpolation section of the XML file. In order to
find the correct destination element/cell for  a given node of the CFD mesh we
use the  Intersecting Sequences  of dD Iso-oriented  Boxes algorithm  from the
Computational Geometry  Algorithms Library (CGAL)  \cite{cgal}. This algorithm
takes two sets of bounding boxes,  does some spatial sorting on them and calls
a callback function for each pair  of intersecting boxes from the two sets. We
use this algorithm by generating axis  aligned bound boxes around the cells of
the acoustic mesh  as well as around  the nodes of the CFD  mesh. The bounding
boxes around the acoustic cells are  generated by find the minimal and maximal
coordinates of the nodes of the  acoustic cell. The bounding boxes for the CFD
nodes  are  generated  by  using  the  global  tolerance  parameter  from  the
interpolation in  a way  shown in Fig.  \ref{fig:interpolation_tolerances}. If
such  a pair  of bounding  boxes intersects  the callback  function  will then
transform the location of the CFD node into the reference coordinate system of
the acoustic  cell and use the  local tolerance parameter to  specify a region
around the reference cell. If the CFD node is within this region the algorithm will
then use the  current acoustic cell as destination cell. The  value of the CFD
node will just be distributed among the nodes of this acoustic cell.

\begin{figure}[htb]
\begin{center}
\includegraphics[scale=1]{pics/interpolation_tolerances}
\caption{Meanings of the tolerance parameters for the interpolation step. \label{fig:interpolation_tolerances}}
\end{center}
\end{figure}

The  algorithm would select  the bounding  box pair  of the  CFD node  and the
acoustic   cell  and   do  a   callback  for   the  example   shwon   in  Fig.
\ref{fig:interpolation_tolerances}.  It would  however reject  to map  the CFD
node to the acoustic cell since the node is outside the local tolerance.

\noindent A good guidline for choosing the tolerances is to use less than half
of the minimal edge length in the acoustic grid as maximal global tolerance so
that  only nodes  really  close to  the  acoustic cells  get  selected by  the
intersecting bounding boxes algorithm. Specifying  a too large value here will
cause lots of unsuccessful bounding box intersection operations. It is however
sometimes necessary to  go up with the  global tolerance to be able  to map on
curved boundaries.   One should choose  as small local tolerances  as possible
since  the the  basis functions  for  weighting the  source value  tend to  be
negative outside an elements domain. Another cause for errors is the fact that
the CFD node can get projected to the wrong destination cell if this tolerance
is to large. The cell neighboring the  current cell my be the correct one, but
the node  already got mapped. This  is why one should  start the interpolation
with  very tight global  and local  tolerances and  iteratively add  CFD nodes
which need larger tolerances. This may  either be achieved by using the start,
end and inc attributes in the XML file or by making use of the restart file or
by combining both possibilities.

\subsection{Important Guidelines}

Allways check  if the sum  of the source  terms on the source  and destination
meshes are equal. The only case when  the sums should not be equal is when you
interpolate  from  a  geometrically   larger  source  domain  into  a  smaller
destination domain. If  the sum over the the source  domain cannot be computed
easily (e.g. when interpolating  just one plane of a 3D dataset  to 2D) try to
evalute the sum for a number  of different destination meshes. In the simplest
case just use one destination element covering the whole source region.
The evaluation of the sum may be performed by reading in the datasets
\begin{verbatim}

\end{verbatim}
on the source and destination HDF5 files and summing them up. This may be done

\subsection{Getting into the Code}

The code for the calculation  of the conservative interpolation is distributed
among the following files:
\begin{verbatim}
source/Forms/acouRHSLinForm.cc
source/Domain/grid.hh
source/Domain/grid.cc
\end{verbatim}
The following methods are of interest:
\begin{itemize}

\item  {\tt   AcouRHSLinForm::CalcElemVector  (acouRHSLinForm.cc)}  Calculates
exactly one entry of the acoustic right hand side vector.

\item  {\tt Grid::ComputeConservativeInterpolationWeights  (grid.cc)} Computes
bounding boxes for source nodes and destination elements for one source region
and calls the bounding box intersection algorithm.

\item {\tt Grid::ConsInterpReportFunctor::operator()  (grid.hh)} Is a callback
for the bounding box algorithm and checks  if a source node is indeed inside a
destination  element. If  this is  the  case it  calculates the  interpolation
weights by evaluating the element  basis functions at the local coordinates of
the source node.

\end{itemize}

\section{Rebuilding the Documentation}

Switch on  {\tt CPLREADER\_REBUILD\_DOCU} in CMake  GUI. Make sure  you have a
recent   TikZ/PGF  distribution   and   Pygments  ({\tt   http://pygments.org}
$\rightarrow$ {\tt sudo easy\_install  Pygments}) installed. Edit the following
files:
\begin{verbatim}
source/cplreader/Documentation/latex/CMakeLists.txt
source/cplreader/Documentation/latex/cplreader_docu.tex
source/cplreader/Documentation/latex/Title.tex
source/cplreader/cmake_options.cmake
\end{verbatim}
To add additional sources edit the  CMakeLists.txt and add them to the list of
CPLREADER\_DOCU\_SOURCES. After doing  so make sure to do a  CMake run so that
the sources  get copied to  the build directory.  This step is  also necessary
after  copying new pictures  to the  pics subdirectory.  Then you  may rebuild
CFS++ and cplreader.  The resulting PDF documentation file  for cplreader will
be copied back to the  source directory automatically. Therefore you just need
to commit the changes to SVN.
